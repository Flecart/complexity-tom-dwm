A Notion of Complexity for Theory of Mind Problems via Discrete World Models

Angelo Huang*, Emanuele La Malfa, Samuele Marro,
Andrea Asperti, Antony Cohn, Michael Woolridge
University of Bologna and Oxford University
EMNLP 2024
Paper Code arXiv

Abstract

Theory of Mind (ToM) can be used to assess the capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) in complex scenarios where social reasoning is required. While the research community has proposed many benchmarks, the inherent difficulty of each problem is unclear and varies largely. This work proposes a framework to measure the complexity of ToM tasks. For a ToM task expressed in natural language, the framework quantifies a problem's complexity as the number of states necessary to solve it correctly. Our measure also accounts for spurious states designed to make the problem harder. We use our method to assess the complexity of five widely adopted ToM benchmarks. On top of this framework, we design a prompting technique that augments the information available to a model with a description of how the environment changes with the agents' interactions. We name this technique Discrete World Models (DWM) and show how it elicits superior performance on ToM tasks where it naturally applies.
